# Intro

## Core Insight
> Our worldview is distorted by 10 dramatic instincts—but replacing gut reactions with data-driven habits reveals a world improving far faster than we perceive.

---

## Representative Samples: 4

### Sample 1: The "Developing World" Myth
**Scenario**: In 1965, 125 countries had child mortality rates above 5%. Today, only 13 do. Yet most people still mentally split the world into "rich West" vs. "poor rest," missing the vast middle-income majority where most humans now live.
**Pattern**: The *Gap Instinct* creates false binaries. Replacing "developed/developing" with four income levels (from $1/day to $80/day) reveals continuous progress—and prevents misallocating aid or overlooking emerging markets.

### Sample 2: Extreme Poverty's Collapse
**Scenario**: In 1800, 85% of humanity lived in extreme poverty. Today it's 9%. Despite this, >90% of people in wealthy nations believe poverty has worsened—because news amplifies crises, not slow, steady gains.
**Pattern**: The *Negativity Instinct* makes improvement invisible. Progress is gradual; drama is instant. Training yourself to ask "What's getting better?" counters the brain's threat bias and reveals actionable optimism.

### Sample 3: Disaster Deaths Down 75%
**Scenario**: Natural disasters kill 75% fewer people today than a century ago—thanks to better infrastructure, early warnings, and emergency response. Yet each earthquake or flood dominates headlines, reinforcing the *Fear Instinct* that the world is getting more dangerous.
**Pattern**: Fear hijacks attention. When a scary event occurs, ask: "How many people *didn't* die because of preparedness?" This reframes risk and highlights solutions worth scaling.

### Sample 4: The 4 Million vs. 14.4 Million Trap
**Scenario**: Hearing "4 million infant deaths last year" feels catastrophic. But comparing it to 14.4 million in 1950 reveals one of humanity's greatest achievements: saving 10+ million children annually through vaccines, sanitation, and education.
**Pattern**: The *Size Instinct* distorts isolated numbers. Always contextualize: compare across time, normalize per capita, or benchmark against goals. Big numbers without reference points paralyze; ratios reveal progress.

---

## Key Generalizations

| Concept | Core Truth |
|---------|-----------|
| Drama Distorts | Our brains prioritize threatening, unusual, or emotional stories—making steady progress invisible unless we deliberately seek data. |
| Context Converts | A number alone misleads; a number compared across time, place, or population reveals truth. |
| Instincts Are Tools | The 10 instincts evolved to keep us safe—not to understand global trends. Use them consciously, not automatically. |

## Formula
> **The Factfulness Filter**: When confronted with a claim, ask:  
> 1) *What's the comparison?* (vs. past, vs. other groups)  
> 2) *What's the trend?* (improving, worsening, stable)  
> 3) *What's missing?* (the silent majority not in the headline)

---

## Conclusion
To see the world factfully, practice stress-reducing skepticism: carry only opinions backed by comparable data. Use Rosling's four income levels, demand context for scary statistics, and celebrate incremental gains. Progress isn't headline-worthy—but it's real.
